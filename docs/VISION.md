# Project Vision

## The Ultimate Automotive Engineering & Manufacturing Simulator

### Vision Statement

> "I don't want to build another car game. I want to build the world's most realistic automotive engineering simulator where every engineering decision matters. Players won't simply customize cars—they will think, design, manufacture, innovate, compete, and evolve exactly like a real automotive company."

The game combines:

* Automotive Engineering
* Mechanical Engineering
* Aerodynamics
* Manufacturing
* Research & Development
* Artificial Intelligence
* Business Management
* Motorsport Engineering
* Product Evolution

into one continuous ecosystem.

---

## The Philosophy

Current games only show **one part** of the automotive world.

| Game | Focus |
|------|-------|
| Automation | Engines |
| Car Company Tycoon | Company management |
| Motorsport Manager | Racing |
| Forza | Driving |
| CAD software | Design |

**This project combines all of them into one interconnected experience.**

Every system affects another. Nothing exists independently.

## The Core Principle

Every engineering decision changes the entire vehicle.

For example, increasing engine bore doesn't just increase horsepower. It also changes:

* Engine size & weight
* Cooling requirements
* Fuel economy
* Compression ratio
* Manufacturing cost
* Reliability & emissions
* Vehicle balance
* Lap times
* Warranty risk

**Everything is connected.**

## The Player Journey

The player begins with an empty company — no cars, no technology, no factories, no reputation — and slowly builds an automotive empire through engineering rather than simply buying upgrades.


## 1. Supply Chain & Procurement

A real automotive company doesn't make every part. The player must build and manage a global supplier ecosystem.

### Tier-1 & Tier-2 Suppliers
- Negotiate contracts with parts suppliers (brakes, glass, electronics, raw metals)
- Choose between in-house manufacturing vs. outsourcing each component
- Supplier quality ratings — cheaper suppliers deliver lower consistency
- Long-term partnerships unlock co-development agreements

### Logistics & Just-In-Time
- Manage inbound parts logistics — shipping routes, warehousing, lead times
- JIT vs. buffer stock strategies (JIT saves cost, buffer protects against disruption)
- Container shipping vs. air freight tradeoffs

### Supply Chain Disruption Events
- Port strikes, natural disasters, geopolitical conflicts halt specific materials
- Single-source dependency risk — if your only brake supplier fails, production stops
- The player can invest in supply chain diversification to mitigate risk

### Vertical Integration
- As the company grows, the player can acquire suppliers
- Own a foundry? Reduce engine block cost but increase capital expenditure
- Own a battery gigafactory? Control cell chemistry and supply

## 2. Talent & Human Resources

People are the most important asset of any engineering company.

### Engineer Hiring & Development
- Recruit engineers from universities, competitors, and motorsport teams
- Engineer specializations: powertrain, aero, electronics, manufacturing, test, data science
- Experience system — engineers improve over time through project exposure
- Burnout mechanic — overworked engineers make more mistakes and may resign
- Star engineers provide breakthrough bonuses but demand higher salaries

### Engineering Culture
- Company culture score (innovation-focused vs. cost-focused vs. quality-focused)
- Culture affects recruitment — top engineers want to work at innovative companies
- Internal knowledge transfer — when a veteran leaves, knowledge walks out the door

### Teams & Departments
- Assign engineers to specific vehicle programs
- Cross-department collaboration bonuses
- Internal competition between vehicle programs for budget and engineers

### Training Programs
- Send engineers to advanced training, conferences, university partnerships
- Training costs time and money but permanently improves capability
- Mentorship programs — senior engineers train juniors faster

## 3. Dealership & Distribution Network

Building a car isn't enough — the player must sell it.

### Dealership Types
- Company-owned showrooms (higher control, higher cost)
- Franchise dealers (lower cost, less brand control)
- Online direct-to-consumer (Tesla model — low overhead but customer resistance)
- Pop-up experience centers in shopping malls

### Regional Distribution
- Build dealership networks region by region (NA, EU, Asia, Middle East, Oceania, Africa, South America)
- Each region has different tastes, regulations, and price sensitivity
- Logistics cost increases with geographic distance from factory

### Customer Experience
- Showroom quality affects conversion rate
- Test drive availability
- Delivery experience (unboxing ceremony, home delivery, dealer pickup)
- After-sales service quality affects brand loyalty and repeat purchase

### Used Car & Certified Pre-Owned
- Player's older models enter the used market — resale value reflects build quality and reputation
- CPO programs generate additional revenue and maintain brand prestige
- Strong resale value improves new-car attractiveness

## 4. Regulatory Compliance & Homologation

Real manufacturers spend billions on compliance. The player should feel this pressure.

### Type Approval Process
- Each vehicle must pass regional homologation before sale
- US (NHTSA/EPA), EU (WVTA), China (CCC), Japan (JARI), etc.
- Different regions have different crash test standards, emissions requirements, and equipment mandates

### Emissions Testing Cycles
- WLTP, EPA FTP-75, China Phase 6 — each cycle measures differently
- A car that passes EU emissions may fail US standards
- The player must design and tune for target markets

### Noise Regulations
- Exterior noise pass-by tests
- Interior NVH targets for different market segments
- EV-specific sound generators (AVAS) required at low speeds

### Recall System
- If a design flaw is discovered post-launch, the player must issue recalls
- Recall cost = (units affected × repair cost) + brand damage
- Proactive recalls cost less reputation damage than delayed ones
- Government-mandated recalls are worse than voluntary ones

## 6. Customer Lifecycle & Ownership

The relationship doesn't end at delivery.

### Warranty & Service
- Design warranty programs (3yr/36k vs. 5yr/60k vs. unlimited)
- Longer warranties build trust but cost more when reliability is poor
- Service network quality affects ownership satisfaction
- Warranty claim patterns reveal engineering weaknesses

### Customer Retention
- Loyal customers buy their next car from you — but only if ownership is good
- Loyalty programs, trade-in incentives, upgrade paths
- Conquest sales — winning customers from competitors is harder than retaining
- Net Promoter Score — tracks word-of-mouth reputation

### Insurance Partnerships
- Work with insurers to offer competitive rates for safer vehicles
- Insurance group ratings affect sales — expensive insurance hurts volume models
- Safety innovations reduce insurance costs and attract buyers

### Aftermarket & Accessories
- Official accessories catalog (performance parts, cosmetic upgrades)
- Aftermarket support generates ongoing revenue
- Performance packages (factory-backed tuning kits)
- Licensing your brand to third parties

### All-Season Capability
- Summer, all-season, and winter tire performance
- AWD/4WD system testing in snow, mud, sand, gravel
- Water fording depth testing
- Towing capability and trailer stability testing

### Environmental Certification
- Achieve eco-labels and green certifications for marketing value
- Life-cycle assessment (LCA) — cradle-to-grave carbon footprint
- Recyclability percentage tracking

## 14. Platform Engineering & Modular Architecture

Real manufacturers share platforms across multiple models.

### Platform Strategy
- Design flexible platforms that underpin multiple vehicles
- A single platform can spawn sedan, SUV, coupe, wagon, and EV variants
- Platform sharing reduces development cost per model dramatically
- Platform age matters — aging platforms need replacement

### Modular Powertrain Architecture
- Design powertrain modules (ICE, hybrid, PHEV, BEV) that plug into the same platform
- Standardized interfaces reduce engineering effort for new variants
- Module refresh cycles — update the powertrain without redesigning the whole car

### Parts Commonality
- Higher parts sharing between models reduces manufacturing cost
- But too much sharing makes models feel "same" — brand differentiation suffers
- Track parts commonality percentage across portfolio

## 17. NVH Laboratory (Noise, Vibration, Harshness)

Premium vehicles are defined as much by what you *don't* hear.

### NVH Engineering
- Engine mount tuning — isolate vibration from cabin
- Exhaust harmonics engineering — tune the "voice" of the car
- Road noise insulation — underfloor deadening, acoustic glass, tire noise
- Wind noise reduction — A-pillar shape, mirror design, door seals

### Sound Quality Design
- Not just reducing noise — shaping it
- Door close "thunk" quality — a luxury signature
- Start-up sound design (EV startup chimes, ICE rumble)
- Active sound enhancement vs. active noise cancellation

### NVH Testing
- Anechoic chamber testing
- Rolling road NVH measurement
- On-road NVH validation at multiple speeds
- Customer NVH complaints feed back into design

## 18. Thermal Management System

Heat kills performance, reliability, and battery life.

### Engine Thermal Management
- Cooling circuit design — radiator sizing, water pump flow, thermostat strategy
- Oil cooling for high-performance engines
- Turbocharger thermal management (heat soak, cool-down)

### Battery Thermal Management
- Liquid cooling vs. air cooling vs. immersion cooling
- Pre-conditioning (heat battery before fast charging in cold weather)
- Thermal runaway prevention
- Battery temperature directly affects range, charging speed, and lifespan

### Cabin Thermal Comfort
- HVAC system design — heat pump vs. resistive heating
- Seat heating/cooling integration
- Solar load management (tinted glass, roof insulation)
- Multi-zone climate optimization

### Brake Thermal Management
- Brake cooling duct design
- Disc material selection (cast iron vs. carbon ceramic)
- Fade simulation under repeated hard braking
- Track-day brake performance validation

## 28. Time Period & Era System

The automotive world of 1960 is different from 2030.

### Technology Availability by Era
- Carburetors and drum brakes in the 1960s
- Fuel injection and ABS in the 1980s
- Hybrid tech in the 2000s
- Solid-state batteries in the 2030s
- Technology unlocks naturally through the R&D tree as time progresses

### Cultural Shifts
- 1960s: horsepower wars, muscle cars
- 1970s: oil crisis, downsizing, regulations
- 1980s: turbo era, electronics begin
- 1990s: safety revolution, JDM culture
- 2000s: hybrid dawn, SUV boom
- 2010s: EV disruption, autonomous driving
- 2020s: full electrification, sustainability pressure
- 2030s+: autonomous mobility, flying cars, hydrogen

### Design Trends
- Period-appropriate design language expectations
- Retro designs in modern eras generate nostalgia premium
- Futuristic designs in early eras may confuse customers

## 30. Lifecycle Management & Model Cadence

Managing a portfolio over decades.

### Model Lifecycle
- New model → mid-cycle refresh (3-4 years) → next generation (6-8 years)
- Refresh scope: new bumpers, updated tech, revised engine tunes
- Full redesign: new platform, new everything
- Lifecycle cost tracking per model

### Portfolio Strategy
- How many models to offer simultaneously
- Which segments to enter or exit
- Cannibalization risk — do your own models compete with each other?
- Halo car strategy — a flagship that lifts the entire brand

### Discontinuation Decisions
- When to kill an underperforming model
- Final edition specials generate last burst of sales
- Orphaned customers need continued parts and service support
- Discontinuation affects brand perception (abandoning a segment = lost trust)
